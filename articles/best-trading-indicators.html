Best Trading Indicators to Spot Market Opportunities: Support & Resistance, RSI, and EMA

August 20, 2025 8 min read

Intraday support and resistance levels, Relative Strength Index (RSI), and Exponential Moving Averages (EMA crossovers) are among the most powerful tools traders use to spot momentum shifts and market reversals. Learn how these indicators work—and how automation can simplify tracking them for multiple tickers at once.

The world of technical analysis is built on patterns, signals, and levels that help traders anticipate price movement. Among the most popular tools are the Relative Strength Index (RSI), Exponential Moving Averages (EMA), and the use of intraday highs and lows as support and resistance zones. Together, these techniques can give traders a clearer roadmap of when to enter or exit a trade.

An essential part of a trader’s toolkit is the observation of intraday highs and lows—this is basically your bread and butter. These price points often act as natural support and resistance levels. In simple terms, support and resistance mark areas where a stock or ETF has changed direction before, suggesting those levels may once again trigger a reversal. When a stock retests its daily low but fails to break lower, that low may serve as support. Similarly, if a stock approaches its daily high and sellers step in, that level becomes resistance. Identifying these zones is critical for intraday traders, as they often mark the battlegrounds where bulls and bears fight for control. It’s important to remember that any point where price makes a full reversal can be considered support or resistance, which sometimes makes these levels tricky to identify. However, not all levels carry the same weight. Support and resistance zones become much stronger when price has reacted to them multiple times, reinforcing their significance.

The RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and strength of price moves on a scale from 0 to 100. Readings above 70 are often interpreted as overbought conditions, while readings below 30 suggest a stock may be oversold. When combined with other indicators, RSI can confirm whether a trend has room to run or if it may be due for a pullback. RSI by itself doesn’t provide much value, but when combined with support and resistance levels, it becomes a powerful tool. For instance, if a stock’s RSI is above 70 (overbought) while the price is approaching a known resistance zone, it’s a strong signal that the stock may be due for a pullback. On the other hand, if RSI dips below 30 (oversold) near a key support level, it suggests the price could be preparing for a rebound.

The Exponential Moving Average (EMA) puts more weight on recent price data, making it more responsive than a simple moving average. Traders often look at EMA crossovers for signals. For example, when a short-term EMA (like the 9-period) crosses above a longer-term EMA (like the 21-period), it’s viewed as a bullish signal, suggesting upward momentum. Conversely, when the short-term EMA crosses below the long-term EMA, it can indicate potential weakness. This “Golden Cross” formation is a recurring chart pattern that commonly signals strength and the continuation of a trend. These crossovers are most effective when used in tandem with other momentum tools like RSI.
